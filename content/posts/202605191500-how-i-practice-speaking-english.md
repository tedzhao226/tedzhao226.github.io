# How I Practice Speaking English

## English

One speaking drill has helped me more than I expected.

The routine is simple:

1. Pick a topic and talk to yourself for two minutes.
2. Record it.
3. Listen back.
4. Use AI to transcribe and evaluate your speech.
5. Try the same speech again with a clearer version.

I learned this kind of drill from a language practitioner.
Their version did not rely on AI, but AI made it easier for me to keep doing it.
I could see what I said, notice awkward phrasing, and rewrite the same idea in cleaner English.

After about 20 days, I felt a clear difference.

The trick is to start with topics you actually use.
For me, tech and work are easier because I already talk about them often.
I can speak more fluently when I have the words ready in my head.
I also rotate topics so the practice does not become too boring.

Another drill I use is retelling my day like a diary.
This helps with casual conversation.
Small talk is difficult for me, but I realised that a lot of small talk is just telling small stories from daily life: what happened today, what was annoying, what was funny, what I noticed.

At the beginning, I found technical topics easier than casual ones.
Explaining technical jargon was hard, but at least the structure was familiar.
Daily stories felt harder because they needed more natural phrasing.

Listening matters as much as speaking, maybe more.
There is a theory about the "silent period" in language learning, where children spend a long time listening before they speak much.
I do not know how far that theory applies to adult learners, but it matches my experience.
Listening builds passive vocabulary, grammar intuition, and a sense of what sounds natural.

For English, I have probably spent thousands of hours watching YouTube.
That helped a lot.
I am not sure whether the same path works as easily for Chinese learners, because interesting Chinese content can be harder to find depending on your taste.
But the principle still holds: you need a lot of input before speaking starts to feel natural.

If you are learning Chinese, I would try the same routine.

Pick one useful topic.
Talk for two minutes.
Record yourself.
Transcribe it.
Ask AI or a native speaker where it sounds unnatural.
Then say it again.

Do that for a few weeks.

Do not practise random textbook topics forever.
Practise the things you actually want to talk about.

## 中文：我是怎么练习英语口语的

有一个口语练习方法对我帮助比预期大。

方法很简单：

1. 选一个话题，自言自语两分钟。
2. 录下来。
3. 回听自己的表达。
4. 用 AI 转写，并让它评价你的表达。
5. 用更清楚的版本再说一遍。

我最早是从一位语言学习实践者那里学到这类练习的。
他推荐的版本不一定需要 AI，但对我来说，用 AI 更容易坚持。
我可以看到自己到底说了什么，哪里表达别扭，然后把同一个意思改成更自然的英文。

练了大概 20 天之后，我能明显感觉到变化。

关键是从你真的会用到的话题开始。
对我来说，技术和工作相关的话题更容易，因为我平时本来就经常聊这些。
当相关词汇已经在脑子里时，我会说得更流畅。
我也会轮换不同话题，不然很容易觉得无聊。

另一个我会做的练习，是像写日记一样复述自己的一天。
这对闲聊很有帮助。
闲聊对我来说一直不容易，但我后来发现，很多闲聊其实就是讲日常生活里的小故事：今天发生了什么，哪里有点烦，哪里有点好笑，我注意到了什么。

一开始，我反而觉得技术话题比日常话题简单。
解释技术术语当然也难，但至少结构比较熟悉。
日常故事更难，因为它需要更自然的表达。

听力和口语一样重要，甚至可能更重要。
语言学习里有一个叫 "silent period" 的说法，意思是小孩在大量开口前，会先花很长时间听。
我不知道这个理论能在多大程度上套用到成年人身上，但它和我的经验是吻合的。
听力会帮你积累被动词汇、语法直觉，以及什么表达听起来更自然。

对我来说，我可能已经看了几千小时的英文 YouTube。
这件事帮助很大。
我不确定学中文的人能不能用同样的路径，因为好看的中文内容可能更难找，取决于你的兴趣。
但原则应该是一样的：你需要大量输入，输出才会慢慢变自然。

如果你正在学中文，我会建议你试试同样的方法。

选一个真正有用的话题。
说两分钟。
录下来。
转写出来。
让 AI 或母语者告诉你哪里不自然。
然后再说一遍。

坚持几周。

不要一直练随机的课本话题。
练你真的想聊的东西。
